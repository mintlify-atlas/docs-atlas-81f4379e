The philosophy behind this hub is simple enough to state in one sentence: build when an idea feels real, solve the actual problem first, and share the result freely because if it helped one person it will probably help someone else. Nothing here was built to look impressive. Everything was built because something wasn't working, and a tool needed to exist.

## The Problem These Tools Address

The central problem is drift. In AI-assisted workflows, drift describes what happens when an AI gradually loses accurate track of the context, constraints, and decisions that should be shaping its responses. It's not dramatic — it's quiet. A constraint gets softened. A decision made two sessions ago gets treated as a hypothesis. A nuanced boundary collapses into a vague general principle. By the time you notice, the session has been operating on a distorted map for a while.

Drift happens partly because AI models have no persistent memory across sessions by default, and partly because the nature of language models is to find coherent continuations — which means they will fill in gaps with plausible-sounding material rather than flagging that something is missing. The result is a tendency to collapse nuance: complex distinctions become simple ones, hedged conclusions become confident ones, and multiple separate considerations merge into a single summary that loses the texture of the original.

Behavioral contracts address this by making the constraints explicit and structural rather than implicit and conversational. Instead of hoping the AI remembers what matters, a behavioral contract tells it exactly which layer of information carries what kind of authority, what should never be collapsed together, and what decisions are fixed versus still open.

## Core Principles

<Steps>
  <Step title="Build for real problems first, then share">
    Every project starts as a solution to a problem that actually exists in practice. The hub contains nothing that was designed to fill a category or demonstrate a concept without a genuine use case behind it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Force useful decisions rather than leaving things open-ended">
    Open-ended AI sessions tend to produce open-ended results. The tools in this hub are designed to force the session toward specific, actionable outputs — a verdict, a next action, a defined constraint — rather than further discussion that leaves everything unresolved.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Human direction is non-negotiable — AI assists, doesn't lead">
    The human sets direction, makes decisions, and owns the outcome. AI tools handle drafting, structuring, reviewing, and surfacing options. This distinction isn't philosophical preference — it's practical. Sessions where the AI leads tend to drift. Sessions where the human leads tend to stay on target.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Keep it operational — rough and working beats polished and unused">
    A protocol that works in a real session with real friction is more valuable than a protocol that looks clean and complete but hasn't been tested against anything. Most projects in this hub are rough in places. That roughness is evidence of use.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Transparency about AI assistance">
    These projects were made with AI assistance. That's stated plainly because the hub is about keeping AI-assisted work honest and legible — and that standard has to apply here too. AI assistance doesn't make a project automatically correct, complete, or right for every situation.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## On AI Assistance

These projects were created through human-directed, AI-assisted work. The ideas, repository materials, practical structure, and decisions about what problems to address were all shaped through that process — with AI tools involved in drafting, structuring, review, testing, and refinement at various stages.

That involvement is acknowledged openly rather than minimized. The goal isn't to claim these projects are purely hand-crafted, nor to suggest that AI involvement makes them somehow more authoritative. It means exactly what it says: a human directed the work, made the decisions that mattered, and is responsible for the output. The AI was a capable and useful assistant throughout.

AI assistance does not make any project automatically correct, complete, or suitable for every use case. Models generate plausible-sounding content, and plausible-sounding is not the same as accurate, well-scoped, or right for your specific situation. That's precisely why protocols like Canon Boundary Guard and Shaping Frame exist — they're tools for catching the gap between plausible and correct before it causes a problem.

## Why Share?

Because if something solved a real problem, there's a reasonable chance it will help someone else with the same problem.

Some of these projects are rough. Some are weird. Some are more serious than they appear at first glance. But they're real, and they come from use rather than from trying to look impressive. A rough tool that solves a genuine problem is more useful than a polished one that doesn't address anything real.

The work gets shared because sharing is the natural end state of solving a problem in public.

<Warning>
  Read it, test it, and adapt it to your own context. These tools solve problems in specific ways — they may not fit every situation. A protocol that works well in one workflow may need significant adjustment before it's useful in another. Treat everything here as a starting point, not a finished answer.
</Warning>
