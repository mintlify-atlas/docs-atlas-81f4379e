Decision Rain Library is a template and system for keeping research and discovered resources actually usable after you find them. It's not a bookmarking tool. It's not a notes dump. It's a lightweight decision-library structure that accepts only reviewed items — links, tools, repos, research signals, and the decisions made from them — and organizes them in a way that makes them retrievable and actionable when you need them next week, not just in the thirty seconds after you found them.

## The Discovery-to-Oblivion Problem

You find a useful tool during a research session. You bookmark it. It joins six hundred other bookmarks in a folder called "Useful" that you haven't opened since last year. Or you paste it into a note. The note is now in a pile of other notes with no structure. Or you link it in a chat thread that expired. Or you just close the tab thinking you'll remember it, and you don't.

This is the discovery-to-oblivion pipeline, and it's where most of the value from research sessions goes to die. The problem isn't that you're not finding useful things — it's that the systems most people use to capture those things (browser bookmarks, notes apps, chat history, open tabs) are not organized around usability. They're organized around capture speed, and capture speed without a retrieval system is just a faster way to lose things.

Decision Rain Library is built around the premise that a small, reviewed, organized library is worth infinitely more than a large unprocessed one.

## What the Library Organizes

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Reviewed Links" icon="link">
    Links that have been opened, read at least partially, and assessed for relevance. Not "might be useful" links — links where you've confirmed what they contain and why they matter to your current work.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Tools & Repos" icon="toolbox">
    Software tools, GitHub repositories, APIs, and technical resources that have been tested or evaluated enough to have a real opinion about. Captured with enough context to remember why they matter.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Research Signals" icon="signal">
    Findings, data points, patterns, and observations from research sessions that point toward a decision or direction. Signals are not conclusions — they're the inputs that lead to conclusions, and they need to stay traceable.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Decisions Made from Research" icon="check-to-slot">
    The actual decisions that came out of evaluated signals and reviewed sources. Keeping decisions linked to the research that drove them prevents the same questions from being re-litigated from scratch in future sessions.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## The "Reviewed" Distinction

The library only accepts reviewed items. This is the design constraint that makes the system work, and it's the one most people want to relax immediately. Don't.

A raw bookmark is a liability, not an asset. It takes up space in your mental inventory of "things I should deal with" without providing any value until you go back and review it. An unreviewed link pile is just a deferred to-do list that makes you feel organized while actually just postponing the work of organizing.

A reviewed item is different. You know what it is. You know why it matters to your current work. You know what decision it informs or what question it answers. That knowledge is what makes the item retrievable — not just findable by keyword, but actually retrievable in context when you need it. The review step is the work that transforms a raw discovery into a usable asset. Skipping it and storing the raw discovery instead is a way of doing the capture work while avoiding the value-creating work.

The Decision Rain Library structure enforces this distinction by making reviewed status a first-class field. If it hasn't been reviewed, it doesn't live in the library.

<Tip>
  Keep the library small and reviewed. A library of fifty items you've actually processed is more useful than a library of five hundred items you haven't. When the library starts growing faster than you can review, that's a signal to stop adding and start processing — or to be honest about which items were never going to be useful and cut them before they crowd out the ones that are.
</Tip>

## Using the Template

<Steps>
  <Step title="Clone or download the template">
    Get the Decision Rain Library template from [github.com/XxYouDeaDPunKxX/decision-rain-library-project](https://github.com/XxYouDeaDPunKxX/decision-rain-library-project) and read the README for the intended structure and field definitions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Set up your four library sections">
    Create the four core sections in your local instance: Reviewed Links, Tools & Repos, Research Signals, and Decisions Made from Research. The structure is lightweight by design — this is not a database, it's an organized file.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Establish your review habit">
    Decide when you'll do review passes. "After every research session" works. "Once a week" works. "Whenever I remember" does not work. The review habit is what keeps the library from becoming another unprocessed pile.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add only reviewed items">
    When you find something useful, do the minimum viable review before it enters the library: open it, read enough to know what it actually contains, write one sentence about why it matters, and file it in the right section. If you can't do that in the moment, add it to a separate staging area for later — not directly to the library.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Link decisions to their signals">
    When you make a decision based on library research, record it in the Decisions section with explicit links to the signals and sources that drove it. Future-you will not remember why you decided what you decided. The link is the memory.
  </Step>
</Steps>
